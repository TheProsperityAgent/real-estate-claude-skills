# Before / After — offer-counter

## A real Greenville counter

**Inputs:**
- Buyer offered $385K on $405K list.
- Seller countered $395K with no other terms.
- Listing agent: Sarah Mitchell (Pinder Team works with her regularly).
- Buyer is firm at $385K — financing supports up to $400K but they want to leave dry powder for inspection items.
- Flex room: close date (buyer has 30-90 day flexibility), can leave the kitchen appliances.

## Before — 9 PM blank-screen reply

> Hi Sarah,
>
> Thanks for sending the counter. We've discussed and unfortunately my buyer cannot meet at $395K. They are a young couple just starting out and this is the absolute max they qualified for at $385K. They are firm — please let the seller know we cannot go higher.
>
> Sorry to make this difficult. Hopefully your seller can accept.
>
> Best,
> Al

What just happened — line by line:
- "Young couple just starting out" — demographic disclosure (FHA-adjacent + bad negotiation).
- "Absolute max they qualified for" — leaks the buyer's financing ceiling. The seller now knows the buyer cannot stretch. Listing agent gains leverage.
- "Sorry to make this difficult" — apologizing for your buyer's position. Weak posture.
- "Hopefully your seller can accept" — passive, no terms-flex offered.

Net effect: weak negotiating position + FHA-adjacent disclosure + no path forward.

## After — offer-counter skill with fair-housing-overlay loaded

> Hi Sarah,
>
> Thanks for getting that back to me. My buyer is going to hold at the original offer on price. We can flex on the close date — they have flexibility from May 25 through end of June, so if a longer runway helps the seller, we can work to that. We're also fine leaving the kitchen appliances in place.
>
> Let me know what your seller is thinking. I'd love to find the middle ground here.
>
> — Al

5 sentences. What it does:
- Holds price firm without sounding hostile.
- Flexes on close date (terms the buyer doesn't care about).
- Flexes on appliances (small concession that costs the buyer nothing).
- Collegial sign-off, leaves room for one more counter.
- Zero demographic disclosure. Zero financing-ceiling leak.

## The seller-side translation

When Sarah Mitchell received this reply and walked her seller through it:

- Seller heard: "buyer is serious, has flexibility on timing, willing to give on appliances."
- Seller asked: "would you offer $390K and call it done?"
- Sarah sent a $390K counter at noon the next day.
- Al replied: $387.5K + buyer eats the title insurance.
- Deal closed at $387.5K.

The "before" version (financing-leak + weak posture) would have ended at the seller's $395K — buyer either matches or walks. The "after" version preserved the negotiation, gave the seller something to say "yes" to (appliances + flexible close), and left $7,500 on the buyer's side.

That's the math the skill protects. One reply email, written well, was worth $7,500 to the buyer.

## Tone notes — the small-market lesson

Notice the after-version never says "my buyer will walk" or "this is firm" or "final offer." Even when the position IS firm, the language doesn't have to be a threat.

Reasons:
1. **Negotiating signal.** "Firm" / "final" / "walking" are signals you keep in reserve. Use them when you actually mean them. Burning them on a soft hold weakens you for the next round.
2. **Listing-agent relationship.** Sarah Mitchell knows Al works with her on 4-5 deals a year. If Al's tone is collegial AND firm, she shows the seller a partner. If Al's tone is hostile, she shows the seller a problem.
3. **The 20-year reputation.** Al has been an agent in Pitt County for years. The listing-agent network reads every counter. One year of "Al's always collegial even when firm" is worth ten new-listing-agent relationships.
